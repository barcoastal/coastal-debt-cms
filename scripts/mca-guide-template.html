Chapter 02

Merchant Cash Advance, explained.

A Merchant Cash Advance (MCA) operates similarly to a payday loan but for small businesses. A business receives a lump sum of capital and pays back a multiple of that amount with daily automatic withdrawals. These advances are often backed by the business's accounts receivable.

MCAs are known for their short-term nature, minimal documentation, and rapid funding sometimes within 24 hours. Typically, smaller and medium-sized businesses with less-than-ideal credit histories resort to MCAs, facing considerably exorbitant rates compared to standard business loans.

Industry Note

In 2016, the aggregate amount of Merchant Cash Advance borrowing reached approximately $10 billion. The default rate on these advances was up to 600% higher than loans issued by the U.S. Small Business Administration.

Finding your way out

Got a Merchant Cash Advance and now feeling the pinch? You're not alone. MCAs can be a quick fix for cash, but they come with a catch: high costs and a piece of your daily card sales. If you want to break free from your MCA, there's a way out and this guide covers the practical steps.

Repayment terms differ from conventional loans. Instead of fixed amounts, repayment occurs through automated electronic deductions: a certain percentage of the borrower's daily, weekly, or monthly card sales is sent directly to the MCA provider until the entire payback amount is settled.

Industry Note

Terminology like "interest rate," "debt," or "loan" is not used when discussing MCAs they're technically classified as advances. Those within the MCA industry justify this by claiming they are not charging interest in the traditional sense, but rather purchasing future revenue from businesses at a discounted rate.

Chapter 03 · Your Options
1

Attempt to renegotiate.

Depending on the status of your account and payments, it could be worthwhile to initiate a conversation with your MCA provider before making any radical financial decisions. Inform them openly that you're facing difficulties. Inquire about lowering daily payments or a short-term pause. An MCA lender's ultimate goal is to recover funds, so they may be open to renegotiation if it increases the likelihood of repayment.

Coastal Debt Resolve Advises

Avoid prematurely informing your cash advance provider about payment struggles. Most contracts include a reconciliation clause meaning if your business income drops, the lender should work with you to adjust the plan. If they don't, they may be breaking your agreement and the loan could be considered predatory. An experienced MCA agreement lawyer can offer valuable guidance.

2

MCA consolidation.

Consolidating MCA debt is similar to general debt consolidation. It involves securing a lower-interest, long-term installment loan to retire one or more high-interest MCAs. You're tasked with repaying the new loan, which offers the convenience of a single, fixed monthly payment.

Theoretically sound, but it comes with credit qualification difficulty. A favorable rate is contingent upon robust credit and if your payment history shows missed payments or an MCA in collections, your credit score has likely suffered. The debt also still exists, just spread over a longer term.

Chapter 03 · Option 3

Debt settlement.

Exploring the option to settle your Merchant Cash Advance debt is a strategy that involves negotiating with the MCA provider to agree on a payment that is less than the total amount due. However, embarking on this journey solo is fraught with risks.

MCA lenders might consent to a settlement if it becomes clear that you cannot meet the original repayment terms. They often prefer a partial recovery of the advance rather than engaging in costly and lengthy legal battles.

A settlement that's negotiated correctly resolves the debt for less than what's owed. A settlement that's negotiated wrong can become a judgment, a lien, or both.

Exploring Bankruptcy

Bankruptcy with legal guidance.

It's important to consult with an attorney who can assess whether your circumstances warrant a Chapter 7 liquidation, an 11 reorganization, or a 13 repayment plan. The specifics differ from state to state, and a filing reflects on your credit for 7 to 10 years.

07 Liquidation

The harsh reality of business liquidation.

A court-appointed trustee takes over your business, tasked with selling assets to meet creditors' demands. Personal guarantees remain intact. The trustee earns a percentage of collected funds, incentivizing them to extract as much as possible.

13 Surrendering Control

A trustee takes charge.

Extra charges like interest and fees are reduced, not the principal you owe. You get a plan to pay over a few years but managing this alone, on top of the cost and shame of bankruptcy, is heavy.

11 The Tough Choice

Reorganize and continue operating.

You give up control to your creditors, and it costs a lot (more than $25,000). Success isn't guaranteed your case could turn from Chapter 11 to Chapter 7, meaning everything might be sold off.

Step 02

Strategic bank account management.

Recognizing the various ways creditors can target personal and business accounts, changing and safeguarding your bank accounts is imperative. Keep the location of these accounts unknown to creditors to prevent potential attachments or garnishments.

After securing your cash, devise a settlement budget to resolve old debts. Consider allocating at least 5% of your total defaulted debt per month.
